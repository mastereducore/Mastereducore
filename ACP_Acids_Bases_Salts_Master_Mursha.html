Part 1 — Basic MCQs

Q1. Farmers usually treat acidic soil in their fields by mixing in:
(a) Quicklime
(b) Alum
(c) Ammonium sulphate
(d) Copper sulphate
Show Answer
(a) Quicklime — it is basic in nature and neutralises excess soil acidity.
Q2. A certain solution turns red litmus paper blue. Adding an excess of which liquid below would reverse this colour change?
(a) Baking soda solution
(b) Lime water
(c) Dilute hydrochloric acid
(d) Ammonium hydroxide solution
Show Answer
(c) Dilute hydrochloric acid — an acid is needed to neutralise the basic solution and turn litmus red again.
Q3. The pH range within which human blood normally stays for the body to function is:
(a) 6.0 – 6.5
(b) 7.35 – 7.45
(c) 8.0 – 9.0
(d) 5.0 – 6.0
Show Answer
(b) 7.35 – 7.45
Q4. A dilute base is slowly poured into a beaker of dilute acid. Which of the following occur?
(i) Temperature of the mixture rises   (ii) Temperature falls   (iii) Temperature stays constant   (iv) A salt is formed
(a) (i) only
(b) (ii) and (iii)
(c) (iii) only
(d) (i) and (iv)
Show Answer
(d) (i) and (iv) — neutralisation is exothermic and produces a salt (+ water).
Q5. A conductivity apparatus (battery, bulb, electrodes) is dipped once in a glucose solution and once in dilute KOH solution. Which statement(s) are correct?
(i) Bulb stays off in glucose solution since it does not ionise   (ii) Bulb glows in KOH solution because it furnishes ions   (iii) Bulb stays off in both because the circuit is incomplete   (iv) Bulb glows in glucose too since it is aqueous
(a) (i) and (ii)
(b) (ii) and (iv)
(c) (iii) only
(d) (iv) only
Show Answer
(a) (i) and (ii)
Q6. When a reactive metal like magnesium reacts with an acid, hydrogen gas is released. How is its presence confirmed?
(a) It turns lime water milky
(b) It burns with a pop sound near a flame
(c) It extinguishes a burning splint
(d) It bleaches moist litmus paper
Show Answer
(b) It burns with a pop sound near a flame.
Q7. Electrodes connected to a battery and bulb are dipped separately in (i) dilute sugar solution (ii) tap water (iii) dilute sulphuric acid, with all other connections complete. In which case(s) will the bulb glow?
(a) (iii) only
(b) (i) and (iii)
(c) (ii) and (iii)
(d) all three
Show Answer
(c) (ii) and (iii) — tap water has dissolved mineral ions; sugar solution has none.
Q8. Four samples P, Q, R, S register pH values of 2, 6, 9 and 13 respectively on a universal indicator strip. Which pair is acidic?
(a) P and Q
(b) Q and R
(c) R and S
(d) P and S
Show Answer
(a) P and Q (pH below 7)
Q9. To safely prepare dilute sulphuric acid from its concentrated form, the correct practice is to:
(a) Add acid to water slowly, with constant stirring
(b) Add water to acid quickly
(c) Mix equal volumes together directly
(d) Add a base first to cool the acid
Show Answer
(a) Add acid to water slowly, with constant stirring.
Q10. Strongly heating limestone (CaCO₃) gives a product 'X' along with CO₂. 'X' is:
(a) Slaked lime
(b) Quicklime
(c) Gypsum
(d) Baking soda
Show Answer
(b) Quicklime (CaO) — CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Q11. Dilute sulphuric acid dropped onto a moist blue litmus strip (held in a delivery-tube set-up) turns it red. This colour change is due to:
(i) presence of an acid   (ii) presence of a base   (iii) H⁺ ions in solution   (iv) litmus acting as an indicator
(a) (i) and (iv)
(b) only (ii)
(c) only (iii)
(d) (i), (iii) and (iv)
Show Answer
(d) (i), (iii) and (iv)
Q12. A corrosive-hazard warning label is compulsorily pasted on containers storing the hydroxide of:
(a) Magnesium
(b) Calcium
(c) Sodium
(d) Aluminium
Show Answer
(c) Sodium — NaOH is highly corrosive.
Q13. Aluminium turnings are warmed in a test tube with concentrated sodium hydroxide solution. The compound formed is:
(a) NaAlO₂
(b) Na₂AlO₄
(c) Al(OH)₃
(d) Na₃AlO₃
Show Answer
(a) NaAlO₂ (sodium aluminate), along with H₂ gas.
Q14. Crystals of washing soda, blue vitriol (copper sulphate) and gypsum all share one common feature — they:
(a) are acidic in nature
(b) are always blue coloured
(c) contain a fixed number of water molecules of crystallisation
(d) conduct electricity in the solid state
Show Answer
(c) contain a fixed number of water molecules of crystallisation.
Q15. Mixing baking soda with a mild edible acid in the presence of moisture releases a gas that makes cake/bread batter soft and spongy. This gas is:
(a) Hydrogen
(b) Carbon monoxide
(c) Carbon dioxide
(d) Oxygen
Show Answer
(c) Carbon dioxide
Exam TipWhenever a question mentions "pop sound" → the gas is always Hydrogen. Whenever it mentions "turns lime water milky" → the gas is always CO₂.

Reason: They remain as molecules in water and do not release ions on dissolving.

Part 3 — Short Answer Type (2/3 Marks)

Q22. A strip soaked in vanilla essence loses its characteristic smell when dipped into a colourless liquid 'Y'. (a) Name the type of indicator vanilla essence represents. If 'Y' turns blue litmus red, describe what you would observe on treating 'Y' with (i) a magnesium ribbon, and (ii) solid calcium carbonate. Write the balanced equations involved.
Show Answer
Vanilla essence is an olfactory indicator — its smell fades in basic solutions but stays unchanged in acidic ones. Since Y turns blue litmus red, Y is an acid.

(i) With magnesium ribbon: brisk effervescence; hydrogen gas is evolved.
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑

(ii) With solid calcium carbonate: brisk effervescence; CO₂ gas evolved, which turns lime water milky.
CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
Q23. Two colourless beakers contain an acid and a base respectively, and no litmus paper is available in the lab. Suggest two different methods (other than litmus) to tell them apart.
Show Answer
1. Natural indicator method: Dip strips of china rose (hibiscus) extract — it turns dark pink/magenta in acid and green in base.
2. Reaction method: Add a pinch of solid sodium carbonate/baking soda to each — brisk effervescence (CO₂ evolved) confirms the acid; the base shows no reaction.
Q24. Explain why an aqueous solution of an acid conducts electricity, while the same acid in its pure/anhydrous (water-free) state does not.
Show Answer
An acid needs water to ionise and release H⁺ (as H₃O⁺) ions, which act as charge carriers. In the absence of water, the acid remains as neutral molecules with no free ions available, so it cannot conduct electricity.
Q25. A student dilutes concentrated nitric acid by pouring water directly into it. Why is this practice dangerous, and what is the correct method?
Show Answer
Dilution is highly exothermic. Adding water to concentrated acid causes the heat to be generated in a small volume of water, which can boil suddenly and splash acid violently — risking burns. The safe method is to add the acid slowly, in small amounts, into a larger volume of water, with constant stirring, so the heat is dissipated safely.
Common MistakeStudents often write "acid is added to water" and "water is added to acid" as if they mean the same thing — in exams, always specify which is added to which; the order is safety-critical and frequently carries marks.

Part 4 — Long Answer Type (5 Marks)

Q26. During the industrial preparation of caustic soda, a gas 'P' is released as a by-product. When 'P' is passed over dry slaked lime, it forms compound 'Q', which is used to disinfect swimming pool water. Identify P and Q, giving the chemical equations involved.
Show Answer
P = Chlorine gas (Cl₂)
Q = Bleaching powder, CaOCl₂

Chlor-alkali process: 2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂
Formation of Q: Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O
Q27. Iron filings are treated with dilute sulphuric acid, producing a gas that is useful in the manufacture of vegetable ghee (hydrogenation of oils). Name the gas, write the balanced equation, and describe a test to confirm it.
Show Answer
Gas evolved: Hydrogen (H₂)
Fe + H₂SO₄ → FeSO₄ + H₂↑
Test: Bring a burning matchstick/splint near the mouth of the test tube — the gas burns with a characteristic 'pop' sound.
Q28. (a) Compound 'M' is used in fire extinguishers and, on heating, converts into compound 'N', which is used to soften permanently hard water. Identify M and N.
(b) Give the chemical formula of Plaster of Paris and state the temperature range at which gypsum is heated to obtain it.
Show Answer
(a) M = Sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃); N = Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃)
2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O

(b) Plaster of Paris: CaSO₄·½H₂O, obtained by heating gypsum at 373–393 K (100–120°C).
Q29. A metal carbonate 'X' reacts with dilute acid to release a gas which, when passed into limewater 'Y', turns it milky and forms back a carbonate precipitate. Separately, gas 'G', collected at the anode during electrolysis of brine, is passed over dry 'Y' to give compound 'Z', which is used to disinfect drinking water. Identify X, Y and Z, with equations.
Show Answer
X = Any metal carbonate, e.g. Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)
Y = Slaked lime, Ca(OH)₂
Z = Bleaching powder, CaOCl₂

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O
Cl₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O

Part 5 — Case Study / Source-Based Questions (5 Marks)

Case Study 1
A universal indicator paper changes colour continuously across the pH scale of 0–14, letting us judge exactly how acidic or basic a solution is, rather than just acidic vs basic.
  1. Solution R is a weak acid and solution S is a weak base. Roughly where on the 0–14 scale would you mark R and S?
  2. Suggest one substance you could add to raise the pH of a neutral solution, and one to lower it.
  3. If the pH of a solution rises from 9 to 11, what happens to its hydroxide ion (OH⁻) concentration, and what colour shift would the pH paper show?
Show Answer
1. R (weak acid) ≈ pH 5–6; S (weak base) ≈ pH 8–9 (close to neutral, unlike strong acids/bases near the extremes).
2. To raise pH: add a base such as sodium hydroxide. To lower pH: add an acid such as dilute hydrochloric acid.
3. OH⁻ concentration increases (by a factor of 100, since pH rose by 2 units); the pH paper colour shifts further towards blue/violet.
Case Study 2
In a diaphragm cell, electrolysis of brine (concentrated NaCl solution) releases gas 'M' at one electrode and gas 'N' at the other electrode, along with an alkaline solution 'L'. Gas N burns with a pop sound; gas M is used for disinfecting water and, when passed over dry slaked lime, gives a solid compound 'K'.
  1. Name gases M and N.
  2. Write the balanced chemical equation for the formation of solution L.
  3. (i) What happens when red litmus solution is added to L? (ii) What happens when CO₂ is passed through an ammoniacal solution of NaCl? (iii) Identify K and write the equation for its formation.
Show Answer
1. M = Chlorine gas (Cl₂); N = Hydrogen gas (H₂)
2. 2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂
3. (i) Red litmus turns blue, since L (NaOH) is basic.
(ii) It forms sodium hydrogen carbonate: NaCl + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃ + NH₄Cl
(iii) K = Bleaching powder (CaOCl₂); Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O
Quick RecallChlor-alkali process always gives 3 products: NaOH (at the cell/solution), Cl₂ (anode), H₂ (cathode) — remember it as "Chlorine anode, Hydrogen cathode, NaOH stays behind."
